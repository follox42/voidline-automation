# Six aircraft and 27 men vanished off Florida in clear weather in 1945. The Navy searched for five days, found nothing, and closed the file with two words: "cause unknown."

I want to focus on the *investigation* into Flight 19 rather than the folklore
that grew up around it, because the documented procedural story is stranger and
more unsettling than the "Bermuda Triangle" version most people know.

**What actually happened.** On the afternoon of December 5, 1945, five TBM/TBF
Avenger torpedo bombers took off from Naval Air Station Fort Lauderdale on a
routine navigation training exercise — a triangular over-water course they had
all flown before. The weather was good. Within about ninety minutes, radio
operators on shore picked up the flight leader saying his compasses had failed
and that he could not tell which way was west. Over the next couple of hours the
transmissions grew more disoriented; the planes seemed to fly further out over
open ocean instead of turning back toward the coast. Then the radio went quiet.

**The rescue plane that vanished too.** A PBM Mariner flying boat with a crew of
13 launched to search for them. It never came back either. A passing tanker
reported an explosion and a slick of oil in roughly the area the Mariner would
have crossed, which is the most likely explanation for that aircraft — the
Mariner had a reputation for fuel-vapor problems and the nickname "flying gas
tank." But that accounts for the rescue plane, not the five it went looking for.

**The search that found nothing.** What followed was one of the largest air-sea
searches conducted up to that point: hundreds of aircraft and surface ships
covering hundreds of thousands of square miles for five days. They recovered no
wreckage, no debris field, no oil slick tied to the Avengers, no life rafts, and
no bodies. Six aircraft and 27 men left no physical trace anyone could find.

**The verdict.** The Navy's Board of Inquiry produced a long report and initially
attributed the loss partly to the flight leader. His family contested it, and the
finding was later revised. The official cause of the loss of the five Avengers was
ultimately recorded as "cause unknown" — an unusually blunt admission for a
military investigation of that scale.

**Why this is genuinely unresolved (and where the myth overreaches).** The honest
mystery is narrow and real: a trained flight of five aircraft became fatally
disoriented in flyable weather, and no wreckage was ever recovered to explain it.
Reasonable candidates exist — a cascade of instrument failure, the leader losing
his frame of reference over featureless water, the flight running out of fuel far
from land at night, and Atlantic conditions that scatter and sink wreckage. None
of those has ever been *confirmed*, because there was nothing to recover. The myth
overreaches by folding it into a supernatural "Triangle"; the documented case
doesn't need the embellishment to be disturbing.

**What I'd love this sub's take on:** given the recovered Mariner explanation, how
much of the "no trace at all" framing for the five Avengers is genuine anomaly
versus the ordinary difficulty of finding downed aircraft in deep open ocean in
1945? Is "cause unknown" here a real evidentiary gap, or just a careful Board
refusing to guess?
