# They had a prime suspect in 1971. A single fingerprint comparison let him walk. Then amateurs — not police — cracked the cipher 49 years later.

I want to talk about the *investigation* into the Zodiac Killer rather than
re-litigate the murders themselves, because the procedural story is arguably
stranger than the case. This is one of the best-documented serial cases in
American history, and yet the two moments that mattered most both cut against
the police.

**The suspect who was cleared on one comparison.** By 1971, Vallejo
investigators had a name they liked: Arthur Leigh Allen. He fit an unsettling
number of the circumstantial markers — the right build, a Zodiac-brand watch
with the crosshair logo, statements witnesses found alarming, and a history
that put him near the cases. But the physical evidence didn't close: latent
prints recovered from a Zodiac crime scene and from the mailed letters didn't
match Allen's. Handwriting comparison didn't clinch it either. On the strength
of those non-matches he was released, and the file effectively went cold around
him for decades. The uncomfortable question the case never resolved: how much
weight should a single fingerprint comparison carry when the prints themselves
may not even belong to the killer (letters pass through many hands; crime scenes
get contaminated)? Allen died in 1992 having never been charged. A later DNA
attempt from letter stamps/envelopes was likewise inconclusive — degraded,
mixed, and never a clean exclusion *or* inclusion.

**The code the police couldn't break — and amateurs could.** The Zodiac mailed
four ciphers. The first (the "408") was solved within days in 1969 by a
schoolteacher couple, Donald and Bethye Harden, working at their kitchen table.
The 340-character cipher ("Z340"), mailed in November 1969, defeated the FBI,
the NSA, and every professional cryptanalyst who tried it for **51 years**. It
was finally cracked in December 2020 by a three-person international team of
*hobbyist* codebreakers — David Oranchak (a software developer), Sam Blake (an
Australian mathematician), and Jarl Van Eycke (a Belgian warehouse worker who
writes cipher-solving software as a hobby). The solution turned out to hinge on
a non-obvious transposition scheme and a diagonal read order — the message was
never gibberish, just scrambled in a way no standard tool assumed. The FBI
confirmed the decryption. It contained no name.

**Why this is the interesting part.** Strip away the fog and you have a case
where the institutions with subpoena power, crime labs, and federal
cryptanalysis units were repeatedly out-performed by private citizens — a
schoolteacher, then a warehouse worker with a laptop. And the one time the
police had a live suspect in the room, the case turned on a forensic comparison
that, by modern standards, may never have been decisive.

Questions I'd put to this sub:

- Was clearing Allen on the 1971 fingerprint comparison sound, given how
  little we can trust the provenance of Zodiac's latent prints? Or was it a
  reasonable exclusion that hindsight is unfairly second-guessing?
- Given that the Z340 solution required an *approach* nobody in officialdom
  tried for half a century, is there a methodological lesson here about how
  cold-case cryptanalysis gets stuck?
- The remaining two ciphers (Z13 "My name is —" and Z32) are short enough that
  a unique solution may be mathematically impossible. Do you think either is
  genuinely solvable, or are they permanent noise?

Not claiming a theory of the killer's identity — I'm more interested in why an
investigation this large kept losing to outsiders. Sources in comments.
